People on Income Support

Department:
Department for Work and Pensions
Last updated:

Source:

Area covered:
England and Wales
Time period:
2011 to 2015

The main facts and figures show that:

  • in 2015, in England and Wales as a whole, an equal percentage (1.5%) of White people and people from Other ethnic groups were claiming Income Support

  • at a regional level, in most areas of England and Wales, a higher percentage of White people than people from Other ethnic groups claimed Income Support

  • between 2011 and 2015, the percentage of claimants from both groups has decreased

Things you need to know

Overall, 1.8% of the population in England and Wales claimed Income Support in 2015. However, ethnicity information was not recorded for 14% of that group.

The remaining 86% of Income Support claimants, whose ethnicity was recorded, were made up of 1.5% of the White population, and 1.5% of the Other ethnic groups population.

Because data is missing for 14% of Income Support claimants, the percentage of claimants in the whole UK population (1.8%) is larger than that for the White and Other groups (1.5%).

What the data measures

This data measures the percentage of people in England and Wales claiming Income Support (IS) and breaks that information down by ethnicity and region. It also looks at changes over time, from 2011 to 2015.

People claiming Income Support may be lone parents looking after a child under 5, pregnant, or acting as a carer. People unable to work because they are sick and disabled may also be claiming the benefit.

Claimants must be between 16 and Pension Credit qualifying age, on a low income, and only able to work a limited number of hours a week.

Students who are not living with a parent, or are themselves parents, may also qualify, as well as refugees learning English.

Why these ethnic categories were chosen

This data comes from two different sources: the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP), and the 2011 England and Wales Census. Because DWP and the 2011 Census use different ethnicity questionnaires, and the information was gathered at different times, it hasn’t been possible to provide data for specific ethnic categories.

For this reason, the data is broken down into two broad categories:

  • White – White ethnic groups (including White British and White ethnic minorities)
  • Other – all other ethnic groups

Methodology and type of data

Type of data

Administrative date

Purpose of data source

The number of people claiming Income Support has been drawn from the Work and Pensions Longitudinal study, an administrative database used for internal management information. The study supports the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) agenda for child poverty, welfare-to-work and retirement income planning policy.

Methodology

The population counts (the denominators) are estimates based from the 2011 England and Wales Census.

Claimant figures are the average number of claimants per month in each year.

As two different sources were used to calculate these figures (Department for Work and Pensions admin data and population estimates based on the 2011 England and Wales Census) claimants cannot be split into lower level ethnic groupings. This is due to different ethnicity questionnaires presented to Income Support (IS) claimants and 2011 census respondents, and these questions being answered at different times.

As the 2011 census (used to estimate the population size for each ethnic group each year) did not include a prefer not to say option for ethnicity, the figures here may be undercounts as White and Ethnic Minority Income Support (IS) claimants who did not report their ethnicity are not included in the percentage for that group. The All ethnicity group includes claimants with an unreported ethnicity. The sum of Ethnic Minority and White claimants therefore does not equal the number of claimants in the All group. In November 2015 14% of claimants did not have an ethnicity recorded.

To qualify for IS you must be between 16 and Pension Credit qualifying age. The whole population aged 16-64 has been used to calculate the percentages here. The number of IS claimants aged over 64 in each region is nil or negligible.

The number of people claiming IS has been drawn from the Work and Pensions Longitudinal study (WPLS). This is a relational database which contains longitudinal (spell-based) information on individual’s benefit histories. A relational database is one which is structured to recognise relations between stored items of information. In practice, this allows individuals to be observed over time as the database is updated with new information.

IS claimants counts are published quarterly. Quarterly figures refer to the number of IS claims on the IS System at the end of the reporting month. Annual monthly averages are the average across the four quarters in a year.

The WPLS is created using administrative data held by the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) and datasets are updated quarterly. The data is at the individual level and individuals in receipt of the relevant benefit appear on that benefit’s dataset.

The Income Support (IS) dataset was merged with another administrative dataset containing personal details to source ethnicity, gender, age and geography for IS claimants.

Each individual on the WPLS has a ‘blurring factor’ associated with them for data protection purposes when counting claimants. The ‘blurring’ of the data ensures that no individual can be identified from the dataset. Blurring can include suppressing small counts, rounding figures to whole numbers, or randomly adding or subtracting 1.

Claimant counts are also published on Nomis. Figures on Nomis differ slightly from those here due to rounding of figures on Nomis. There is a larger discrepancy between the November 2015 count presented here and the equivalent statistics presented on Nomis. For November 2015 the differences are small; less than 1 per cent at regional level.

Rounding

Rates rounded to the nearest 0.1
